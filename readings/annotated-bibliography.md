# AI Ethics and Moral Philosophy: Annotated Bibliography

This repository contains my annotated bibliography for AI Ethics and Moral Philosophy. It brings together sources, reflections, and key themes that shaped my thinking about artificial intelligence, moral responsibility, justice, accountability, trust, and human control.

## Overview

This project explores a central idea: artificial intelligence should not be treated as the primary moral agent in ethical debates about AI. AI systems do not have human moral understanding, intentions, or accountability. Instead, they reflect the values, incentives, assumptions, and power structures of the people and institutions that design, train, deploy, and govern them.

Because of that, this annotated bibliography focuses less on fear of AI as an independent moral threat and more on the human issues behind it, especially power, governance, inequality, accountability, and fair access.

## Main Research Focus

This bibliography is organized around several major questions:

- Is AI morally neutral, or does it reflect human values and priorities?
- Who is responsible when AI systems cause harm?
- Can AI ever be morally trusted?
- How do algorithmic systems reinforce inequality and social injustice?
- When does “AI ethics” represent real responsibility, and when does it become empty branding?
- How should AI be governed so that it supports dignity, fairness, and meaningful human choice?

## Core Argument

My overall view is that the deepest ethical problems in AI are usually not about AI acting as a moral being. The deeper problem is how humans use AI inside systems of power.

AI can amplify injustice, hide responsibility, and make harmful systems look objective or efficient. For that reason, ethical discussions about AI should stay focused on human accountability, transparent governance, social impact, and the protection of human dignity.

## Key Themes

### Human Accountability
One of the strongest themes in this project is that AI can create agency without responsibility. Systems can act in the world and affect people’s lives, while the humans behind them try to avoid blame by shifting responsibility onto the technology.

### Moral Agency
This bibliography reflects the view that AI is not a true moral agent. It does not possess human moral understanding, ethical intention, or lived experience. Responsibility must remain with the people who create and control these systems.

### Trust and Moral Limits
Another major theme is trust. AI may be useful or reliable in some contexts, but moral trust requires accountability, ethical boundaries, and meaningful human oversight. Trust cannot simply be claimed because a system appears intelligent or effective.

### Algorithmic Injustice
This project also examines how AI systems can reinforce existing inequalities. Bias is not just a technical flaw. It often reflects social conditions, institutional priorities, and unequal distributions of power.

### Social Justice and Human Dignity
AI ethics is not only about technical design. It is also about whether people are able to live with dignity, participate in society fairly, and maintain real control over decisions affecting their lives.

### Misuse of “AI Ethics”
This bibliography also reflects skepticism toward shallow or performative uses of the term “AI ethics.” Ethics language can become meaningless when it is used for public image without producing real structural change.

## How to Use This Repository

This repository is meant to show how different texts in AI ethics and moral philosophy connect to one another. Each annotated entry identifies the source’s central argument, ethical importance, and relevance to broader debates about AI.

The bibliography can be read in two ways:

1. **Source by source**, to understand each author’s contribution
2. **Theme by theme**, to see how questions of accountability, justice, trust, bias, and governance connect across texts

## Applied Connection

This bibliography also informs a broader practical interest in ethical system design, including ideas connected to AI Guardian Assistant Security Systems and Networks. In that context, the philosophical questions explored here become design questions:

- How should AI-supported systems handle power responsibly?
- What safeguards are needed to preserve human oversight?
- How can AI systems be useful without becoming coercive?
- What does trustworthy design actually require?

In this way, the bibliography is not only a record of sources. It is also a foundation for thinking about how ethical ideas should shape real-world AI and security systems.

## Why This Project Matters

AI is often discussed as if it were simply a technical innovation. This project takes a different view. AI is also a moral, social, and political issue. It affects who has power, who gets heard, who gets excluded, who bears risk, and who is held responsible when harm occurs.

That is why philosophy matters here. Ethical reflection helps move the conversation beyond what AI can do and toward what AI should do, who it should serve, and what limits should govern its use.
