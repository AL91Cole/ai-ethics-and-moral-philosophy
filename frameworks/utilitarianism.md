# Utilitarianism and AI

Utilitarianism is an ethical framework that judges actions by their consequences. In simple terms, it asks whether an action produces the greatest good for the greatest number of people.

## How It Applies to AI

In AI ethics, utilitarian thinking often supports systems that improve efficiency, reduce harm, lower costs, or increase access to useful services.

Examples include:
- AI used to detect disease earlier
- AI used to improve disaster response
- AI used to reduce traffic deaths
- AI used to make services more widely available

## Strengths

Utilitarianism is useful because it focuses on outcomes. It asks practical questions such as:
- Does this AI system reduce harm?
- Does it improve well-being?
- Does it benefit society overall?

## Weaknesses

A major criticism is that utilitarianism can justify harm to minorities if the majority benefits. An AI system might be considered “efficient” overall while still being unfair or harmful to specific groups.

For example, an AI hiring tool might improve speed and reduce costs for a company, but still discriminate against qualified applicants from marginalized groups.

## Ethical Tension

The main tension is that a beneficial outcome for many people does not always make a system morally acceptable if it violates fairness, dignity, or rights.

## Conclusion

Utilitarianism is helpful in AI ethics because it highlights consequences and social impact. However, it should not be used alone. Ethical AI also requires fairness, accountability, and respect for persons.
